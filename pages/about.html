ABOUT

The Peninsula doesn't grade on a curve.

Neither do we.

Run Mad Maps is a trail intelligence platform built for the Cape Peninsula, South Africa. Not a GPS tracker. Not a route aggregator. Not a global platform with a local footnote. A ground-up system that grades Peninsula routes against Peninsula-calibrated standards, scores athletes honestly, and tells you whether you're ready for what you're about to attempt.

The Problem

Global platforms break on this terrain

The Cape Peninsula is not a normal trail environment. 65 named peaks sit in a geography so compact that a single morning run can take you over terrain most platforms would classify as mountaineering. A 5 km route can involve 380 metres of elevation gain and scrambling sections that require your hands. A 2 km gorge route can climb at over 200 metres per kilometre.

Most platforms grade the Peninsula the same way they grade a rolling parkland in Surrey or a forest trail in Oregon. The same algorithms, the same thresholds, the same assumptions about what "moderate" means.

On the Peninsula, those assumptions break. A route graded "moderate" elsewhere can involve sustained 25% gradients, no flat recovery, and terrain that sits in the top percentile of anything on the mountain. An athlete who trusts that rating can find themselves in serious trouble inside the first kilometre.

The problem isn't that no one tracks trail running. The problem is that no one understands this specific terrain well enough to grade it honestly, score athletes fairly, and tell them whether they're ready for what they're about to attempt.

Route Grading

Graded from the GPS. Nothing else.

Every route receives three outputs. A Difficulty Class from A through F — A is the hardest, intentionally. A-grade athletes run A-grade routes. A Terrain Difficulty Score from 1 to 10 for granular discrimination within each class. And an Effort Descriptor that communicates how a route is hard — Flat, Sustained Climb, Relentless Ascent, Front-Loaded.

The full grade reads like this:

Class A · 10/10 — Relentless Ascent · 4.2 km · 888m gain · 212 m/km

That single line tells an experienced athlete almost everything they need to know before they start. Two routes can both grade A — but one is a 6/10 and the other is a 10/10. That distinction matters. It's the difference between a hard day and a route that will test everything you have.

Runner Performance Score

One number. No hiding.

RPS is a single score from 0 to 100. Five components, weighted to reflect what actually matters for trail performance on Peninsula terrain — how far you're running, how efficiently you move across terrain, how much elevation you're accumulating, how consistent your pacing is, and how frequently you're training.

The score operates on a 90-day rolling window with continuous decay. Recent training counts more than old training. If you stop, your score drops — because fitness that isn't maintained isn't real.

Speed efficiency is adjusted for terrain difficulty. Pace consistency is normalised against gradient. Running slower on a steep climb isn't inconsistency — it's physics. The system knows the difference.

Level RPS Meaning
Foundation 0–20 Starting out or returning
Active 21–50 Building a base
Athlete 51–70 Serious, demonstrable fitness
Competitor 71–85 Race-ready performance
Elite 86–100 Peak Peninsula performance

You know where you stand. You know what moves the needle.

Race Readiness

Are you ready? A real answer.

Knowing your fitness score is useful. Knowing whether it's enough for a specific route is actionable. The Race Readiness Engine runs five checks against your 90-day training history for any target route on the Peninsula — distance coverage, training volume, performance intensity, recency, and elevation.

Five checks. Three verdicts.

READY All five pass. Your training supports this. Go.
CLOSE Four of five pass. One specific gap. You know exactly what it is.
NOT YET More preparation needed. Every gap is identified.

Not a vague "you're almost there." A percentage to each threshold. You can see precisely what training will close the gap.

Data Integrity

If the scoring isn't honest, nothing else matters

Every activity runs through a validation pipeline. GPS anomalies, impossible location jumps, split activity detection, fabricated traces, speed inconsistencies. Activities that fail are excluded or flagged for manual review. No automated flag results in a penalty without human review.

Leaderboard data comes from official RMM-timed events only. Training data never enters competitive rankings. The leaderboard is earned, not gamed.
